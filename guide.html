Marking Console

Guide · v1.1.1 Open the app →

A tracker for working through a large marking load: per paper, per section, across classes and devices.

The tool offloads two things only: memory (which mistakes have I already seen) and drafting (the wording of feedback). Every judgement stays with you.

Quick start

  1. Set up (top right) → add a class: give it a name and add your roster, one per line as first name and last initial (e.g. Sophia K). Full surnames are never stored.
  2. On the dashboard, + New marking job → pick the class, name the assessment, set its due date.
  3. Resume the job and start marking. Tick each paper; the dashboard keeps score and paces your days.

Recipes — pick a situation

More

Several jobs with different deadlines

You have 11PSC's test due Sunday and 13PHY's due Tuesday. Which should you start marking today, and how much of each?

In the app

Give each job its own due date when you create it. On the dashboard, Today's quota splits the day across your jobs and orders them by urgency: the Sunday job gets the larger share and sits at the top of the list, with its own small bar of done-versus-target for today.

Work down that list from the top and you stay on track for every deadline without doing the arithmetic yourself. A job with no due date has nothing to pace against, so it will not appear in the quota split; add a due date to any job you want the day planned around.

An assessment with sections

The test has Sections A, B and C, and you want to hold one consistent standard across all of Section A before moving on to B.

In the app

When you create or edit the job, put each section on its own line in the Parts box. Leave it blank and the assessment stays a single whole-paper mark, exactly as before.

Marking then runs section by section: pick a part tab, mark that section down the whole class, and finishing it jumps you to the next section that still needs work. Marking one section across everyone in a row is what keeps your standard steady; that consistency is hard to hold when you mark whole papers one student at a time. Progress and the daily quota count each student-section as one unit, so the numbers reflect the real work.

A mistake you keep seeing

Half the class has dropped significant figures, and you want to both record it per student and plan a reteach.

In the app

In the Trip hazards column on the right, type the mistake once, then click it on each paper where you see it. The list sorts by how often you have logged it in the section you are marking, so the common slips rise to the top as you go.

Copy hazard summary gives you the frequency table to plan the reteach. Keep the wording consistent, for example always "sig figs dropped" rather than a new phrasing each time, so the counts stay meaningful and, later, transfer cleanly into your question bank.

Writing a feedback comment

You want a short, honest comment for a student, without writing every one from scratch.

In the app

On the student's paper, press Copy feedback prompt, then paste it into whichever AI assistant you use — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, any of them.

The prompt carries only the trip hazards and notes you logged for that student, grouped by section, and asks for roughly 80 words, encouraging but honest, inventing nothing beyond what you noted. Always read and adjust the result before it reaches the student; the drafting is offloaded, the judgement is not.

A student was away

One student was absent on the day and you will chase it up; another sat out the whole unit and that is already settled.

In the app

On the paper, choose Absent — follow up for the first. It shows in amber and is counted on the dashboard, so it stays on your radar until you deal with it. Choose Not sitting for the second: it is kept quiet, muted in the roster and off the dashboard, so a settled case never nags you.

Both keep the student out of your percentages and daily quota. When a followed-up student finally sits, open their paper, press Mark present, mark the work, and set Sat on to the day they actually sat.

Flagging for moderation

A borderline script you want a colleague to second-check before marks are final.

In the app

On the paper, press Flag for moderation and add a note on why it is borderline. Flagged students show a ⚑ in the roster.

Every flag and note is gathered into Copy assessment data, giving you a ready moderation record to hand to a colleague. Remove the flag once the case is resolved.

Fixing a tick

You ticked the wrong student, or a mark changed after moderation and needs to go back to unmarked.

In the app

Click the status box next to a name in the roster to tick or untick it in place, or use Un-mark on the open paper. Nothing is ever locked, so you can correct freely at any point.

Marking on two devices

You start on your laptop and want to finish on your phone at a cafe, without losing anything.

In the app

Open Set up → Sync across devices. Create a GitHub token (classic, with the gist scope), paste it in, and press Sync now. The first device creates a private store for your data.

On the phone, paste the same token and the gist id it shows you. From then on it syncs when you open the app and shortly after you edit, merging both ways: marking different papers, or different sections, on each device all survives. You can turn sync off any time, and your data stays on the device regardless.

Keeping a record or analysing results

You want a record of how an assessment went, or data to analyse or compare across classes.

In the app

Copy hazard summary gives the frequency table of mistakes for reteaching. Copy assessment data gives a full, anonymous dump of one assessment: every paper broken down by section, with its hazards, notes and any moderation flags, and papers numbered with no names.

Because it carries no names, you can file it or paste it into an AI assistant and ask questions like "how did this assessment go?" or, when the same test was sat by more than one class, "where did the classes differ?"

Privacy

Student names live only in your browser and, if you switch on sync, in a secret (unlisted) GitHub gist. The app file and the public website contain no student data, which is why they are safe to host and to hand around.

You enter only a first name and a last initial, and that is all that is stored; if you paste a full surname it is reduced to its initial. Where two students would share a label, the app numbers them and you can rename either from the Set up class editor, so you never need to store a full surname to tell them apart.
